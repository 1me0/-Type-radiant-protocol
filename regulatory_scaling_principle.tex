\small An \(\alpha>1+\beta\) Invariant for Adaptive Stability, Collapse, and Resilience}

\date{}

\begin{abstract}
We present a single, dimensionless inequality that governs the survival of any adaptive system: the systemic load
\[
\Lambda(t)=\frac{1+\beta(t)}{\alpha(t)}<1,
\]
where \(\alpha>0\) is the regulatory capacity and \(\beta\ge0\) the disturbance amplification factor.
Static stability requires \(\Lambda<1\); dynamically, \(\Lambda(t)\) is preserved below the critical threshold iff
\[
\boxed{\;\frac{\dot\alpha}{\alpha} > \frac{\dot\beta}{1+\beta}\;} .
\]
We ground this meta‑principle in a minimal two‑variable dynamical model, prove its Lyapunov stability, and derive an explicit collapse time estimate.
The phase diagram comprises stable, critical, and collapse regimes, and we illustrate the theory with simulations.
Corollaries include the Systemic Death Law (\(\Lambda\ge1\) with no return) and the Zero‑Legitimacy Principle (\(\alpha=0\Rightarrow\) immediate collapse).
The framework unifies Ashby's requisite variety, Schrödinger's negative entropy, Fisher's fundamental theorem, and Minsky's financial instability hypothesis under a single invariant.
Falsifiable predictions are stated, and the principle is shown to be a universal mathematical boundary between order and dissolution.
\end{abstract}

\section{Definitions}
\label{sec:def}
Let \(\alpha(t)\ge0\) denote the \textbf{regulatory capacity}—the fraction of internal activity devoted to corrective feedback (controller gain, immune response, renewal rate).
Let \(\beta(t)\ge0\) denote the \textbf{disturbance amplification factor}—the degree to which external perturbations are magnified over one characteristic time scale (leverage, volatility, environmental hostility).
Both are dimensionless; their ratio defines the central quantity.

\begin{definition}[Systemic Load]
\[
\boxed{\;\Lambda(t) \equiv \frac{1+\beta(t)}{\alpha(t)}\;} .
\]
\end{definition}
The numerator \(1+\beta\) is the total disorganizing pressure; the denominator \(\alpha\) is the available regulatory counter‑force.

\section{Static Stability}
\label{sec:static}
\begin{theorem}[Static Stability]\label{thm:static}
A system is locally asymptotically stable iff \(\Lambda(t)<1\), i.e.
\[
\boxed{\;\alpha(t) > 1+\beta(t)\;}.
\]
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The effective amplification gain is \(G = \alpha(1+\beta)\). When \(G<1\), the Subcritical Gain Contraction Theorem guarantees exponential error decay. The inequality \(G<1\) is equivalent to \(\alpha>1+\beta\).
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Critical Threshold]
\(\Lambda=1\) marks the boundary of stability; any crossing with \(\dot\Lambda>0\) leads to collapse.
\end{corollary}

\section{Dynamic Persistence Law}
\label{sec:dynamic}
The static condition is necessary but transient. To avoid the lethal boundary, the load must decrease whenever it is dangerously high.
Take the logarithmic derivative:
\[
\frac{d}{dt}\ln\Lambda(t) = \frac{\dot\beta}{1+\beta} - \frac{\dot\alpha}{\alpha}.
\]

\begin{theorem}[Dynamic Persistence]\label{thm:dynamic}
Assume \(\Lambda(t_0)<1\). Then \(\Lambda(t)<1\) for all \(t>t_0\) if
\[
\boxed{\;\frac{\dot\alpha}{\alpha} > \frac{\dot\beta}{1+\beta}\;}
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\frac{d}{dt}\ln\Lambda(t) < 0 .
\]
If \(\frac{d}{dt}\ln\Lambda(t)\ge 0\) over the system's characteristic response time \(\tau\), then \(\Lambda\) crosses unity, and the system collapses.
\end{theorem}

\section{Minimal Dynamical Model}
\label{sec:model}
To embed the principle in concrete dynamics, we propose a generalised Lotka‑Volterra system capturing the mutual inhibition between regulation and disturbance:
\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\frac{d\alpha}{dt} &= r\alpha - k\alpha\beta + u(t),\\[4pt]
\frac{d\beta}{dt} &= s\beta - m\alpha\beta + \xi(t),
\end{aligned}}
\]
where \(r,s>0\) are intrinsic growth rates, \(k,m>0\) are cross‑inhibition coefficients, and \(u(t),\xi(t)\) are external inputs. All parameters have dimension \(T^{-1}\), ensuring dimensional consistency.

\subsection{Equilibrium and Lyapunov Function}
Without external forcing (\(u=\xi=0\)), the nontrivial equilibrium is
\[
\beta^* = \frac{r}{k},\qquad \alpha^* = \frac{s}{m}.
\]
The equilibrium systemic load is \(\Lambda^* = \frac{1 + r/k}{s/m}\). Stability of the equilibrium requires \(\Lambda^*<1\), i.e.
\[
m\left(1+\frac{r}{k}\right) < s .
\]

Take \(V(\Lambda) = \ln\Lambda\). Along trajectories of~\eqref{eq:dynamic_law},
\[
\dot V = \frac{\dot\beta}{1+\beta} - \frac{\dot\alpha}{\alpha} .
\]
If \(\dot V < 0\), the load decreases monotonically; this provides a rigorous Lyapunov stability proof for the abstract condition.

\section{Phase Diagram and Simulation}
\label{sec:phase}
The theory defines three definitive regimes (Figure~\ref{fig:trajectories}):
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \textbf{Stable regime}: \(\dot\alpha/\alpha > \dot\beta/(1+\beta)\) → load decreases.
    \item \textbf{Critical boundary}: \(\dot\alpha/\alpha = \dot\beta/(1+\beta)\) → constant load.
    \item \textbf{Collapse regime}: \(\dot\alpha/\alpha < \dot\beta/(1+\beta)\) → load grows; once \(\Lambda\ge1\), collapse is unavoidable.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Collapse Time Estimate}
For the simple exponential case \(\alpha(t)=\alpha_0 e^{g_\alpha t}\), \(\beta(t)=\beta_0 e^{g_\beta t}\) with \(g_\alpha < g_\beta\), the inequality \(\dot\alpha/\alpha < \dot\beta/(1+\beta)\) eventually holds.
Solving \(\Lambda(t)=1\) gives the death time:
\begin{equation}
t_{\text{death}} \approx \frac{\ln\frac{\alpha_0}{1+\beta_0}}{g_\beta - g_\alpha},
\end{equation}
provided \(\beta_0\) is small or \(\alpha_0>1+\beta_0\) initially. This quantifies the life expectancy of a collapsing system.

\section{Universality and Corollaries}
\label{sec:universal}
The principle applies identically to any field where regulation and disturbance interact:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \textbf{Cybernetics} (Ashby): \(\alpha\) = controller variety, \(1+\beta\) = environmental variety.
    \item \textbf{Thermodynamics}: \(\alpha\) = negative entropy influx, \(\beta\) = internal entropy production.
    \item \textbf{Evolution} (Fisher): \(\alpha\) = adaptive evolution rate, \(\beta\) = environmental decay rate.
    \item \textbf{Economics} (Minsky): \(\alpha\) = stabilisation policy, \(\beta\) = market speculation.
    \item \textbf{Cognitive regulation}: \(\alpha\) = prefrontal inhibition, \(\beta\) = amygdala reactivity.
    \item \textbf{Institutional viability}: \(\alpha\) = governing capacity, \(\beta\) = institutional decay.
\end{itemize}

\begin{corollary}[Systemic Death Law]
A system dies if and only if \(\Lambda \ge 1\) and the dynamic condition cannot be restored. Death is the irreversible crossing of \(\Lambda=1\) under \(\dot\Lambda \ge 0\).
\end{corollary}

\begin{corollary}[Zero Legitimacy = Zero Response]
If \(\alpha \to 0\), then \(\Lambda \to \infty\) regardless of \(\beta\). The system loses all ability to respond and collapses immediately:
\[
\boxed{\;0\ \text{Legitimacy} \;=\; 0\ \text{Response}\;}.
\]
\end{corollary}

\section{Falsifiable Predictions}
\label{sec:falsifiable}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \textbf{Load monotonicity}: For any system with \(\Lambda(t_0)<1\) and \(\dot\alpha/\alpha > \dot\beta/(1+\beta)\), the load must decrease monotonically over time.
    \item \textbf{Critical slowing down}: Near \(\Lambda=1\), recovery time from perturbations diverges, producing a characteristic early‑warning signal.
    \item \textbf{Phase transition}: As \(\dot\alpha/\alpha\) approaches \(\dot\beta/(1+\beta)\) from above, the system exhibits increasing variance and flickering between stable and unstable regimes.
    \item \textbf{Unavoidable collapse}: No system can survive indefinitely when \(\dot\alpha/\alpha < \dot\beta/(1+\beta)\); the crossing of \(\Lambda=1\) is mathematically inevitable.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
The Regulatory Scaling Principle condenses the essence of adaptive persistence into the single dimensionless load \(\Lambda = (1+\beta)/\alpha\) and the logarithmic stability condition \(\partial_t\ln\Lambda < 0\). The minimal dynamical model, Lyapunov proof, collapse time estimate, and falsifiable predictions transform this principle into a rigorous, testable, and universal framework. It provides a single mathematical boundary between order and dissolution—a true meta‑law of complex systems.
